## Burnout Isn’t Just Being Tired

In tech and security, burnout is becoming a bigger risk than half the CVEs on your dashboard. Chronic overload, constant alerts, and “just one more incident” slowly move you from stressed to *cooked*. [web:193][web:195][web:200][web:203][web:206]

Burnout is more than a rough week. It’s long‑term exhaustion, cynicism, and that hollow feeling where the work you used to care about now just feels pointless. Left alone, it trashes your health, your judgement, and your career. [web:193][web:195][web:199][web:200][web:205]

---

## Spotting Burnout Before It Wipes You Out

Common signs in security/tech roles include: [web:193][web:194][web:195][web:199][web:200][web:205][web:206]

- You dread opening Slack, email, or the SIEM.
- You feel permanently wired and exhausted at the same time.
- Small incidents feel like huge personal failures.
- Your tolerance for colleagues (and family) drops through the floor.
- You’re making uncharacteristic mistakes — in configs, reviews, or incidents.

Remote and hybrid work make this worse because boundaries blur and everything becomes “just a quick check” that never ends. [web:199][web:202][web:205]

If this sounds familiar, you’re not “weak”; you’re human, under sustained load.

---

## Practical Ways to Burn Out Slower (Or Not at All)

You can’t fix everything around you, but you can adjust how you work, recover, and push back. Evidence‑based strategies that actually help: [web:193][web:195][web:196][web:199][web:200][web:202][web:205]

### 1. Tighten Your Work Boundaries

- Set **hard stops**:
  - Pick a finish time and defend it most days (with rare, explicit exceptions).
  - Use calendar blocks and status messages (“Heads‑down, back at 3pm”) so people know. [web:199][web:202]
- Create a shutdown ritual:
  - Close editor/terminal, write tomorrow’s top 3 tasks, close laptop, leave the room. [web:199][web:202]
- Separate spaces:
  - If you work from home, have a distinct work zone — even if it’s just a particular chair or a converted closet. [web:199][web:202]

### 2. Change How You Handle the Pile of Work

- Move from reactive to deliberate:
  - Start the day with one **high‑impact task**, not inbox roulette. [web:199]
- Limit context‑switching:
  - Set “alert triage windows” instead of jumping at every ping. [web:195][web:196][web:197][web:206]
- Break work into small, finishable chunks:
  - Micro‑tasks give you a sense of completion, which combats that “never done” feeling. [web:199][web:202]

### 3. Look After the Meat Computer

- Sleep and movement:
  - Consistent sleep and regular exercise are some of the strongest protectors against burnout and anxiety. [web:199][web:200][web:202]
- Micro‑breaks:
  - Short, frequent breaks (get up, stretch, walk) reduce cognitive fatigue and error rates. [web:199][web:200][web:202]
- Simple self‑care:
  - Do normal, non‑tech things you actually enjoy — hobbies, time outside, canal walks, sport. [web:199][web:200][web:205]

### 4. Use Your People

- Talk early:
  - Raise workload issues with your manager before you hit the wall. A decent manager would *rather* adjust scope than lose you entirely. [web:193][web:194][web:199][web:203][web:205]
- Peer support:
  - Trusted colleagues, community groups, or specialist orgs (e.g. Cybermindz) are a big protective factor. [web:200]
- Professional help:
  - If anxiety, sleep, or mood are sliding hard, a GP, therapist, or counsellor is not overkill — it’s maintenance. [web:199][web:205]

## The Hidden Burnout Engine: Toxic and Draining People

Sometimes it’s not just the work that exhausts you; it’s certain people. You know the ones:

- The perpetual critic.
- The drama generator.
- The “black hole” who absorbs time and emotion and gives nothing back.

Research and practice both show a single toxic person can drag down team performance and morale significantly. [web:201][web:204][web:207]

You may not be able to get rid of them, but you can **limit their impact on your brain and your day**.

---

## How to Deal with Negative, Draining People (Without Becoming One)

Think of this as “emotional RBAC” and “network segmentation” for your attention. [web:198][web:201][web:204][web:207]

### 1. Detach First: Don’t Take the Hook

- Observe instead of absorb:
  - Treat their behaviour like a specimen in a lab or a character on a reality show: “Interesting, they’re doing *that* again.” [web:198][web:204]
- Pause before responding:
  - Breathe, count to five, answer slower than your irritation wants you to. [web:204][web:207]
- Separate behaviour from identity:
  - “They are being toxic *in this moment*” is easier to handle than “They’re a monster and I’m trapped.”

Detachment isn’t apathy; it’s not letting their storm decide your weather.

### 2. Set Clear, Boring Boundaries

You don’t need dramatic speeches. You need short, repeatable lines. [web:198][web:204][web:207]

Examples:

- With a constant complainer:
  - “I’ve only got five minutes, then I have to jump back into this ticket.”
  - After a few minutes: “I need to get back to this now.”
- With someone undermining you:
  - “I hear you. Here’s what I’m trying to achieve. What specifically would you change?”
- With someone pushing work onto you:
  - “My plate’s full with X and Y. Which of those should I drop if I take this on?”

Use the “broken record” technique when needed: calmly repeat your boundary if they push. [web:204]

### 3. Stick to Facts, Not Drama

Toxic people thrive on emotional fog. You cut through it with specifics. [web:198][web:204][web:207]

- “In yesterday’s stand‑up, you said X about my work. Here’s what I actually did. Let’s talk about the difference.”
- “In the last three sprints, I picked up 6 production issues; I can’t take ownership of this one as well without dropping something else.”

Facts make gaslighting and blame‑shifting harder.

### 4. Reduce Their “Network Access” to You

You can’t always avoid them, but you can **reduce surface area**.

- Time‑box interactions:
  - Short, agenda‑driven meetings instead of open‑ended chats.
- Route around where possible:
  - Use documented processes and shared channels instead of one‑to‑one where they dominate.
- Escalate carefully:
  - If behaviour crosses lines (bullying, harassment, sabotage), document dates, times, specifics and go through the proper channels (manager, HR). [web:204][web:207]

Think of this as applying zero‑trust principles to your emotional bandwidth.

### 5. Maintain Your Own Emotional Hygiene

You can’t stop every negative interaction, so your **recovery routines** matter. [web:198][web:199][web:200][web:202][web:204][web:205]

- Post‑interaction reset:
  - Short walk, breathing exercise, music — anything that signals “that’s over; now we move on.”
- Journalling:
  - A quick note: what happened, how you felt, how you’d like to respond next time. It clears headspace and builds emotional intelligence. [web:204]
- Stay grounded in other relationships:
  - Invest in supportive people inside and outside work. One draining colleague feels smaller when you’re not isolated. [web:199][web:200][web:205]

## When It’s Time to Leave

Sometimes the healthiest boundary is the exit door.

If despite your best efforts:

- Your boundaries are constantly ignored.
- You’re routinely undermined or disrespected.
- Burnout signs are worsening, not easing.

…then stepping away — changing team, company, or even domain — is not failure; it’s self‑preservation. [web:193][web:194][web:200][web:205][web:206]

Good orgs are starting to recognise burnout as a **business risk**, not a personal weakness, and are simplifying tools, reducing alert noise, and watching wellbeing as closely as they watch threat dashboards. [web:193][web:195][web:196][web:197][web:203][web:206]

If yours isn’t there yet and shows no sign of moving, you’re allowed to find one that is.

---

## Final Thought

You don’t get extra points in life for running yourself into the ground so someone else’s backlog looks better.

Preventing burnout isn’t just bubble baths and mindfulness apps. It’s:

- Drawing lines around your time and energy.
- Designing work in a way your nervous system can actually sustain.
- Learning to handle draining people with clarity and boundaries, not endless arguments.

Look after the human running the shell. Everything else — your code, your lab, your career — depends on that one staying online.
